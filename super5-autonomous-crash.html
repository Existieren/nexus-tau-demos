The Autonomous Vehicle Safety Challenge

The autonomous vehicle market is projected to exceed $2 trillion by 2030. Companies like Waymo, Tesla, Cruise, and Mobileye are racing to deploy vehicles that can navigate complex urban environments without human intervention. The fundamental promise: autonomous vehicles will be safer than human drivers.

But there is a fundamental verification challenge that no amount of road testing can solve. A self-driving car encounters billions of possible scenarios — combinations of weather, traffic, pedestrians, construction zones, sensor states, and timing. Statistical testing (even billions of simulated miles) can only sample this space. It cannot prove the absence of lethal edge cases.

The core problem is not that individual safety rules fail. Each rule — emergency braking, pedestrian priority, V2V communication — can be verified correct in isolation. The danger lies in the temporal interactions between rules: the brief moments when one rule deactivates and another has not yet taken effect. These gaps are measured in milliseconds, invisible to simulation, and lethal in practice.

Industry Context: How AVs Are Verified Today

The autonomous vehicle industry uses a patchwork of testing and verification approaches. Each addresses part of the safety challenge, but none checks the temporal interactions between multiple safety rules simultaneously.

◆ Waymo (Alphabet)

Uses "Simulation City" — millions of simulated miles per day — combined with extensive real-world testing in Phoenix, San Francisco, and Los Angeles. Their simulation replays real-world encounters with variations. Strong on scenario coverage, but simulation is inherently sampling-based: it tests specific scenarios, it does not prove absence of all gaps.

SIMULATION + ROAD TESTING

◆ Tesla (FSD)

Relies on "shadow mode" and fleet learning: millions of vehicles generate real-world data, and neural networks are trained on edge cases. No formal verification of rule interactions. The approach is powerful for learning common scenarios but cannot guarantee coverage of rare temporal edge cases that have never been observed.

FLEET LEARNING + NEURAL NETS

◆ Cruise / GM

Extensive scenario-based testing with structured scenario libraries. Uses both simulation and controlled real-world testing. Scenario libraries are manually curated from real incidents and expert analysis. The limitation: scenarios are enumerated, so unknown combinations of rule transitions remain untested.

SCENARIO-BASED TESTING

◆ Mobileye (Intel) — RSS

Responsibility-Sensitive Safety (RSS) is the closest to formal methods in production. RSS defines mathematical rules for safe following distances, right-of-way, and occlusion handling. However, RSS checks pairs of agents (ego vehicle + one other). It does not compositionally verify timing interactions across multiple rules simultaneously.

FORMAL RULES (PAIRWISE)

◆ ISO 21448 — SOTIF

Safety of the Intended Functionality is the automotive standard for handling scenarios where the system behaves as designed but still causes harm. SOTIF categorizes scenarios into known-safe, known-unsafe, unknown-safe, and unknown-unsafe. The goal is to minimize the unknown-unsafe region. But SOTIF is a process framework, not a verification tool — it guides what to test, not how to exhaustively check rule interactions.

SAFETY STANDARD (PROCESS)

◆ ISO 26262 — Functional Safety

The foundational automotive functional safety standard. Defines ASIL levels (A-D) for hardware and software. Focuses on preventing random hardware failures and systematic software faults. Excellent for component-level safety but does not address emergent behavior from correct-but-interacting subsystems — the exact class of failure this demo illustrates.

FUNCTIONAL SAFETY STANDARD

◆ NHTSA ADS Framework

The U.S. National Highway Traffic Safety Administration's Automated Driving Systems framework provides voluntary guidance for AV developers. Covers object detection, fallback performance, crashworthiness, and post-crash behavior. Important for regulatory compliance but does not mandate formal verification of temporal rule compositions.
